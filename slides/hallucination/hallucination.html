Two answers, one preference
  • AI produces two answers to a prompt
  • Human picks which they prefer — repeated millions of times
  • Polished, confident answers tend to win
  • On objective tasks (math, code), we cut out the human — and AI races ahead
Here's the training loop that built today's AI. <> The model produces two answers; a human picks the better one; you repeat it millions of times. Sounds reasonable — but notice what's missing: the human often can't check whether either answer is actually *true*. They just pick which sounds better. And humans systematically prefer confident answers over hedged ones. So we're literally training the model: when in doubt, sound sure. <> Now — on tasks where we CAN check, like math or working code, we don't need the human at all. We let the model try, run the check, reward what passes. That's where AI is racing ahead the fastest. <> The flip side: where we can't easily check, the model has been trained to bullshit confidently. That's the seed of hallucination, and we'll have it for as long as we favor "confident" over "correct."

Make it check, not guess
  • Search — let it search the internet, company docs, or your personal files
  • Compute — calculator, code, math: work the problem, don't just guess
  • Human review — slowest, costliest, still the most reliable filter
  • AI cross-check — ask two AIs; agreement is evidence, not proof
  • None eliminate hallucination. Architect the workflow, not just the prompt.
So if hallucination is structural, what do we do about it? Four families, none perfect. <> Let the model search — the internet, company docs, your own files. It reads before it answers, citing what it found. Give it real compute — calculator, code interpreter, math tools. Now it works the problem instead of guessing. Human review on high-stakes outputs — slowest, most expensive, still the most reliable filter. And ask two AIs the same question — if they agree, that's evidence it's probably right, but not proof: they can share blind spots. All four pull the model from "guess what sounds right" toward "verify before answering." None make hallucination go away — they make it rare enough that the system is useful. The takeaway is architectural: trust comes from the workflow, not the model itself.
